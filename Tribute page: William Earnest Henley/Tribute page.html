Who was William Ernest Henley?


William Ernest Henley was an English poet, critic and editor of the late victoria era.

From the age of 12, Henley suffered from Tuberculosis, a disease which resulted in the amputation of one of his legs. While in hospital, Henley wrote many 'hospital poems', which have since become famous.

His most famous 'hospital poem' is 'Invictus', written in 1875. The poem explains that as humans, we experience pain, sufferring and challanges throughout our lives. However, we can strive to overcome these challanges and accept our fates, no matter the hurdle.

The ideas laid out within 'Invictus' strongly reflect elements of stoic philosophy, and the victorian drive to keep a 'stiff upper lip'. The poem has now gone on to inspire millions around the world.

Poetry





Invictus

Out of the night that covers me,
Black as the pit from pole to pole,
I thank whatever gods may be
For my unconquerable soul.

In the fell clutch of circumstance
I have not winced nor cried aloud.
Under the bludgeonings of chance
My head is bloody, but unbowed.

Beyond this place of wrath and tears
Looms but the Horror of the shade,
And yet the menace of the years
Finds and shall find me unafraid.

It matters not how strait the gate,
How charged with punishments the scroll,
I am the master of my fate,
I am the captain of my soul.

The Full Sea Rolls And Thunders

The full sea rolls and thunders
In glory and in glee.
O, bury me not in the senseless earth
But in the living sea!

Ay, bury me where it surges
A thousand miles from shore
and in its brotherly unrest

I'll range forever more.
